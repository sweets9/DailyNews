**Critical RCE Flaw in Trend Micro Apex Central**
A critical remote code execution (RCE) vulnerability, identified as CVE-2025-69258, has been disclosed in Trend Micro Apex Central. The flaw has a CVSS score of 9.8, indicating a high severity. This vulnerability could allow an attacker to execute arbitrary code on the affected system, potentially leading to a complete compromise of the network management platform.
*Why it Matters*: Apex Central is used by many large organizations to manage their security products. A compromise of this system could allow attackers to disable security controls, deploy malware, and move laterally across the network.
*Link*: https://thehackernews.com/2026/01/12/critical-rce-flaw-in-trend-micro-apex-central.html

**VMware Zero-Day Vulnerabilities Under Active Exploitation**
Three zero-day vulnerabilities in VMware products, discovered in December 2025, are reportedly being actively exploited in the wild. The vulnerabilities could be used by attackers to gain initial access to corporate networks, potentially leading to widespread ransomware attacks. The specific products and versions affected have not been fully disclosed, but VMware is urging customers to apply all recent patches.
*Why it Matters*: VMware products are ubiquitous in enterprise environments. The active exploitation of these zero-days means that many organizations are currently at high risk of a security breach. The potential for ransomware attacks makes this a particularly urgent threat.
*Link*: https://thehackernews.com/2026/01/12/vmware-zero-day-vulnerabilities-under-active-exploitation.html

**AI and Supply Chain Risks Top 2026 Cybersecurity Agenda**
The World Economic Forum's latest report indicates that artificial intelligence is significantly accelerating cybersecurity risks. A large majority of cybersecurity leaders believe that AI will be the most significant factor shaping the threat landscape in 2026. Alongside AI, supply chain vulnerabilities remain a primary concern, with many organizations struggling to manage the risks associated with third-party vendors and suppliers.
*Why it Matters*: The increasing use of AI in cyberattacks will make them more sophisticated, harder to detect, and more damaging. At the same time, the complex web of modern supply chains provides a broad attack surface for malicious actors. Businesses need to prepare for a new generation of threats and re-evaluate their supply chain security.
*Link*: https://itbrief.co.uk/story/ai-and-supply-chain-risks-top-2026-cybersecurity-agenda

**UK Proposes Expanded Cyber Security Regulations**
The UK government is considering a new Cyber Security and Resilience Bill that would expand the scope of current regulations to include managed service providers (MSPs), data centers, and other critical suppliers. The proposed legislation would introduce stricter incident reporting requirements and give regulators greater enforcement powers.
*Why it Matters*: This legislation will have a significant impact on any company doing business in the UK. Organizations will need to review their security practices and be prepared for increased regulatory scrutiny. Failure to comply could result in substantial fines.
*Link*: https://www.the-european.eu/story-30013/a-new-era-for-uk-cyber-security-and-resilience-bill.html

**Cyber-Fraud Now a More Common Threat Than Ransomware**
A recent study shows that cyber-fraud, particularly phishing, has surpassed ransomware as the most frequently reported type of cybercrime. Invoice and payment fraud, along with identity fraud, are also on the rise. This indicates a shift in tactics by cybercriminals towards direct financial theft.
*Why it Matters*: While ransomware remains a major threat, businesses must also be vigilant against fraud. This requires a different type of defense, focusing on employee training, robust verification processes for financial transactions, and multi-factor authentication.
*Link*: https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/cyber-fraud-overtakes-ransomware/

**Many Cybersecurity Awareness Programs Are Ineffective**
A new report suggests that many public sector cybersecurity awareness programs are failing to reduce risk because they are not being treated as a formal security control. The report argues that for awareness training to be effective, it must be tailored to specific job roles, based on real-world threats, and integrated into a broader risk management framework.
*Why it Matters*: Organizations spend significant resources on security awareness training. If these programs are not effective, it represents a waste of money and, more importantly, leaves the organization vulnerable to human error. A more strategic and data-driven approach to awareness is needed.
*Link*: https://www.safeharboursecurity.com/news/are-your-cybersecurity-awareness-programs-failing/
