**Record Number of Zero-Day Flaws Targeted Enterprises in 2025**
A new report from Google's Threat Intelligence Group found that nearly half of all zero-day vulnerabilities (flaws with no available patch) discovered in 2025 were aimed at enterprise technology. Attackers are increasingly focusing on security and networking hardware, often called "edge devices," which are difficult to monitor. The report also noted a significant rise in attacks coming from commercial surveillance vendors—companies that sell hacking tools to governments.
Why it Matters: This represents a major shift in the threat landscape. Attackers are moving beyond common targets like web browsers and focusing on critical business infrastructure. Companies must increase scrutiny on the security of their network hardware and cannot assume these devices are safe.
Link: https://securityweek.com/enterprise-targeted-zero-day-exploits-hit-all-time-high-google/

**Cisco Warns of Actively Exploited Flaws and Issues Dozens of Patches**
Cisco has confirmed that two vulnerabilities in its Catalyst SD-WAN products are being actively exploited by hackers. These flaws could allow an attacker to gain control of an affected system. In separate advisories, the company released patches for 48 other vulnerabilities across its widely used firewall and security management products.
Why it Matters: Any organization using the affected Cisco products is at immediate risk from the actively exploited flaws. The large number of other patches indicates a broad effort to fix security holes that could be targeted in the future. System administrators should prioritize applying these updates to protect their networks.
Link: https://securityweek.com/cisco-warns-of-more-catalyst-sd-wan-flaws-exploited-in-attacks/

**CISA Adds Five New Vulnerabilities to "Must-Patch" List**
The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) has added five new security flaws to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog. The list includes vulnerabilities in products from Apple, Hikvision (a video surveillance product manufacturer), and Rockwell Automation. A KEV listing means there is concrete evidence that the vulnerability is being used in real-world attacks.
Why it Matters: CISA's KEV catalog serves as a critical alert system for all businesses. If a product your company uses appears on this list, it means you are facing a current and direct threat. Patching these flaws should be treated as an emergency.
Link: https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog

**LexisNexis Confirms Data Breach After Hackers Leak Files**
Financial and legal data aggregator LexisNexis has confirmed it was the victim of a data breach. The confirmation came after a hacking group claimed to have stolen 2 gigabytes of files, including records containing the personal information of 400,000 people.
Why it Matters: LexisNexis holds vast amounts of sensitive personal and business data, making it a high-value target. A breach of this nature could lead to identity theft, financial fraud, and other crimes using the stolen information.
Link: https://securityweek.com/new-lexisnexis-data-breach-confirmed-after-hackers-leak-files/

**Iranian State Hackers Reportedly Scanning Security Cameras to Aid Missile Strikes**
According to a report from Risky Biz, Iranian state-sponsored hackers are actively scanning for internet-connected security cameras. The intelligence suggests this is being done to gather information to assist with physical missile targeting.
Why it Matters: This news highlights the frightening convergence of cybersecurity and physical warfare. A compromised security camera could not only lead to a data breach but could also facilitate a real-world physical attack, posing a direct threat to personnel and facilities.
Link: https://risky.biz/

**Major Phishing-as-a-Service Platform Dismantled**
An international law enforcement operation has taken down a major cybercrime service known as "Tycoon 2FA." The platform sold a toolkit that made it easy for criminals to launch sophisticated phishing attacks that could bypass two-factor authentication. The service was used to target over 500,000 organizations.
Why it Matters: While this takedown is a victory, it highlights the professionalization of cybercrime. Phishing remains one of the biggest threats to enterprise security, and services like Tycoon 2FA make it possible for even low-skilled attackers to run effective campaigns.
Link: https://securityweek.com/tycoon-2fa-phishing-as-a-service-platform-dismantled/
